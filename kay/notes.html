3 Computers, networks and education (1991)

Computers, networks and education (Scientific American, 1991)

"Gell-Mann has remarked that education in the 20th century is like being taken to the world's greatest restaraunt and being fed the menu. He meant that representations of ideas have replaced the ideas themselves; students are taught superficially about great discoveries instead of being helped to learn deeply for themselves. In the near future, all the representations that human beings have invented will be instantly accessible anywhere in the world on intimate, notebook-size computers. But will we be able to get from the menu to the food? Or will we no longer understand the difference betwteen the two? Worse, will we lose even the ability to read the menu and be satistifed just to recognize that it is one?"

Some of the most effective people I know are super-skimmers and integrators who can glue lots of stuff together. They'll have great project ideas, and be able to glue together extant materials to make those projects come to life easily.

To try to sharpen that up: there's an integration ability that involves the cultivation of skimming and these relatively shallow representations that Kay is talking about.

I'm reminded of Adler's "How to read a book". He makes the point that it's possible to be good at reading a book in 10 minutes, 10 hours, 10 days, 10 years. This is the ability to read 100 books in 10 hours, and to integrate them to create something new. It's a kind of remix-ability, or something like that.

Perhaps it makes sense to cultivate this ability at search and integration as well as the kind of in-depth understanding that Kay is talking about.

I don't think that integrative ability has ever been named. Synoptic ability? Integrative ability? I think it's defined by a mixture of integrative ability, good search ability, taste, and some other qualities. It'd be nice to name this, and to understand what exactly it involves.

"In the Open School… the machines do not substitute for teachers. They are thought of as "just another material," like books, paints and clay, that can support the children's activities."

I like this. It's taking the computer as medium of expression seriously.

There's an old question about whether discovery-based learning is the right way to go. The debate tends to set things up in rather polar views: all discovery-based versus sage-on-a-stage (or similar) models. That's misleading. What is undoubtedly true is that more discovery-based learning is a good idea.

"we are quite capable of devising new mental bricks, new ways of thinking, that can enormously expand the understandings we can attain. The bricks we develop become new technologies for thinking."

"the need for struggle should not be grounds for avoidance. Difficulty should be sought out, as a spur to delving more deeply into an interesting area. An educational system that tries to make everything easy and pleasurable will prevent much important learning from happening."

It's interesting that in many parts of life – sport and video games come to mind – making things easy also makes them unpleasurable. We love to struggle, under some circumstances.
